God. I felt like death. My head felt congested, like a giant mucus membrane. My whole body ached, my head, my arms, my legs. I felt hot and cold. My throat was sore from the coughing and sneezing. It hit me like a train. And I couldn’t understand what I’d done to deserve this. I just felt weak. I didn’t want to get up.   
I picked up the phone and called my boss.
“Hey,” I croaked. 
“What’s up, Daniela?” Cynthia asked me.
“I don’t think I can make it in today.”
“You sound awful. What is it?”
“I don’t know but my body hates me right now.”
“Alright. I know you’re not just blowing me off. Let me know if you need more sick days.”
“Thanks.” I hung up. 
I never missed work; not because I loved it, but because I always felt like I needed to be there and make myself useful. I hated staying at home. I felt useless when I wasn’t working. And since I was sick, I didn’t even feel like doing chores or picking up around the house. I just wanted to stay in bed and sleep. At least it was Friday; I wouldn’t need too many sick days.
I guess I could admit I was feeling a little bit delirious. I stood up to get some water and some saltine crackers, hoping to avoid getting up too many times. I held on to the walls as I walked back to bed. I turned on some shows on Netflix but my tired brain didn’t stay awake for too long. I quickly and wearily fell asleep.   
---
After a couple of hours, I awoke to a knock on my door.
I exhaled and stood up, putting on a robe to keep my warmth. I walked slowly, hoping whoever it was would leave in the time it took me to get to the door. But the knocking continued. So I dragged my feet to the door and opened before peeping through the door. 
My cheeks warmed immediately when I noticed it was Tyler. He worked in my office in a different department. He had a bag in his hand.
“I heard you were sick,” he said and held up the bag. 
I smiled at him. “Thanks. You really didn’t have to.”
“I wanted to.”
I felt woozy again and stumbled around the door. He put an arm around me and helped me to my own couch. I frowned. 
“I’m fine, Ty,” I said to him. 
“You’re running a fever,” he said, pulling my feet up on the couch. He pulled a chair from the dining room. “I bet you haven’t eaten.”
“I ate last night.”
“It’s almost 6pm.”
I looked at the clock on the wall. Indeed it was. I slept for longer than I thought. 
He pulled out a Styrofoam container full of chicken noodle soup. He scooped a spoonful and brought it to my mouth. I swallowed after a bit of chewing. 
“You really don’t have to do this,” I said to him, blinking back tears. 
“I do.” He smiled at me and poured another spoonful into my mouth. 
He continued to feed me and keep me company. It was nice to have some form of support. I smiled at him. 
“Thanks,” I whispered.
“Nothing to thank me for. You brought me lunch every day for like a month after my dad died.” He wiped off a bead of sweat from my forehead. 
“Hey, your fever broke!” He rubbed my hand. “You’re going to be okay.”
“Told you.”
