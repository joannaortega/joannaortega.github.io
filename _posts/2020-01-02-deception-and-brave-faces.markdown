Pardon the hiatus. Nanowrimo kicked my behind straight into 2020. 



Tabitha had a hard day at work today. She always took it pretty hard when she lost one of her animals. But being a vet, she didn’t have much of a choice otherwise. Like doctors lost patients, so would she. Today was natural causes, but it was a dog she’d known for the better part of a decade. So I would bring the wine, her favorite was white, and some fries from Chick-fil-a. I knew exactly what would happen when I arrived. Her eyes would be teary. Her face would be streaked. And then we would play a Meg Ryan classic while we would cry together and eat. Then we would light a candle for the dead animal she’d cared for over time. Ridiculous and out there? Maybe. But she felt each pet that she saw as family.

So I drove to Tabitha’s apartment, taking the bags out of the car and trying to take everything with me at the same time. I knocked on the door trying not to bang the bottles of wine together too hard. 

She opened the door but her face wasn’t all streaky and blotchy. Her green eyes were clear of stormy nights. I couldn’t help feeling taken aback.

“Hey,” I said uneasily.

“Hi, Laura, come in!” she said. She was almost cheerful.

I winced as I walked in, feeling completely baffled.

I walked into the living room to various fistfuls of confetti. 

“Surprise!” a couple of people yelled.

Oh god. I hated surprise parties. 

Tabitha loved throwing them. 

Just…swallow your emotions, I thought. She meant well.

“What is this about?” I asked.

“Your new job! Congratulations!” she yelled. 

I waved at our mutual friends. They were dressed up while I was here, in sweatpants. 

“Did the dog even die?” I asked her as she took the bags from my arms.

“Nope.”

“I won’t believe you next time!” 

She laughed. “Yeah you will. So proud of you, gorgeous.”

“Thanks.” I walked into her bedroom area and went to her closet. I pulled out a dress of mine that I’d left here some months ago. I slipped into it quite quickly and put down my hair from its messy bun. I smeared some of her lipstick on my lips and some mascara on my eyelashes. She hadn’t had any eye diseases. I spotted some tequila on her nightstand and took a swig. 

Tabitha popped her head through the door. “Ready?”

I gave her a reassuring smile. I knew she meant well. 

In my very comfortable sneakers, I walked out, exhaling to greet the masses.
